Research

My research focuses on Empirical Industrial Organisation and Structural Econometrics. I develop new tools to study economies of scope, market competition, and firm behaviour in multiproduct settings.

Working Papers

Identifying Economies of Scope in Multiproduct Firms

Single-authored

Draft available upon request

Presented at: UCL-CeMMAP Econometrics Research Day (2025), EARIE Summer School (2025), UCL Applied Micro Research Day (2024). Scheduled: EAYE Annual Meeting, Bilbao (2026); Econometric Society Asian Meeting, Hong Kong (2026).

Estimating economies of scope is empirically challenging when firms exercise market power and input sharing across products is unobservable. This paper proposes a structural framework for identifying and estimating economies of scope in multiproduct firms by exploiting the duality between production and cost functions. The approach recovers the multiproduct production frontier and implicit cost structure without requiring product-specific input allocations, therefore delivering a theory-consistent scope measure while avoiding the limitations of standard cost-function approaches. I establish nonparametric identification under regularity conditions, and validate the estimator via Monte Carlo experiments. The method is applied to linked French administrative and production microdata to conduct merger and breakup counterfactuals.

Customer-Focused Entry Deterrence: Creating Loyalty in Dynamic Markets

with Sean F. Ennis

Draft available upon request

Companies in growing industries regularly invest in customer loyalty, often appearing loss-making in the short term. We show that such investments can enhance a leading firm's profits not only by expanding its customer base but also by deterring competitor entry. In rapidly growing markets, an incumbent that fails to implement a loyalty programme risks displacement by an entrant who does. We find that as market growth increases, incumbents have stronger incentives to undertake profitable exclusion investments, and that when customers have heterogeneous attitudes toward loyalty programmes, the investment becomes less attractive under explosive growth.

Auditor Concentration after a Regulatory Experiment: Mandatory Audit Firm Rotation

with Konstantinos Bozos and Sean F. Ennis

Draft available upon request

In 2014, the EU mandated that Public Interest Entities regularly rotate their audit firms, aiming to reduce audit market concentration. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design with US PIE-equivalent firms as controls, we find that mandatory rotation failed to achieve this objective: Big 4 and Big 6 fee-weighted market shares increased by approximately 2.1 and 1.3 percentage points relative to the US control group. A flow decomposition reveals that 94% of fee volume among compelled rotators switches within the same network tier, predominantly Big 4 to Big 4. We further document an unintended behavioural response: firms that voluntarily rotated prior to the regulation reduce their switching probability by 26 percentage points. Mandatory rotation thus reshuffles audit relationships substantially but preserves the existing market structure.

Work in Progress

No Firm-Level Output for a Multiproduct Firm: What Production Function Regressions Identify

with Áureo de Paula

Multiproduct firms have no well-defined physical output at the firm level, yet researchers routinely construct scalar output measures and estimate production functions to recover markups via the Hall (1988) formula. We show that different aggregate output constructions yield inconsistent markup estimates, each recovering a different weighted average of product-level markups. Revenue regressions and demand-inversion approaches tend to be uninformative. Informative constructions — quantity indices and price-index deflation — produce weights shaped by marginal input reallocation rather than cost shares. Monte Carlo experiments show deviations of 5–15% from the cost-share benchmark at baseline parameter values, and substantially larger when products are more asymmetric. These distortions are orthogonal to transmission bias, which implies that measured markup trends may partly reflect aggregation artifacts.
